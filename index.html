The Potential Spread of the African clawed frog (Xenopus laevis) in Western Washington

Picture of an African clawed frog © Brian Gratwick retrieved from WSDFW. The name is derived from the claws on the hind feet.

The African clawed frog (Xenopus laevis) is a species native to Africa. Used in scientific experiments around the world and as a pet, the improper release of these frogs has resulted in invasive populations that jeopardize local biota. Within the last decade the African clawed frog has been found in three cities in Washington State: Lacey, Bothel, and Issaquah.

Why it Matters

  • With individuals growing up to 5.5 inches long, African clawed frogs can eat anything they can fit into their mouth including other frogs.
  • The African clawed frog can spread the deadly Bd fungus which has killed amphibians worldwide jeopardizing native species.
  • The African clawed frog carries exotic parasites which could spread in native bird species.
  • A dramatic reduction in reproduction rates among native amphibians has been observed following the introduction of African clawed frogs in other countries source.

Note: The African clawed frog has been known to travel up to 2 km. (about 1.24 mi.) away from its starting point source.
